\section{Level-set risk framework}
\label{sec:framework}

\subsection{An explicit working posterior}
\label{sec:posterior}

For a fixed image, write \(p=(p_1,\ldots,p_N)\). We use a shared random
threshold to construct a law over masks.

\begin{definition}[Level-set working posterior \(Q_p\)]
\label{def:levelset}
Let \(T\sim\operatorname{Unif}(0,1)\) and define
\begin{equation*}
  Y_T=\{i:p_i\geq T\},
  \qquad
  Y_t=\{i:p_i\geq t\}.
\end{equation*}
The distribution of \(Y_T\) is denoted by \(Q_p\).
\end{definition}

The construction satisfies
\begin{equation}
  \Prb_{Q_p}(i\in Y_T)=p_i,
  \qquad
  \Prb_{Q_p}(i,j\in Y_T)=\min(p_i,p_j).
  \label{eq:levelset-marginals}
\end{equation}
Thus \(p\) is exactly the one-pixel coverage function of \(Q_p\), while the
common threshold selects the comonotone coupling of those marginals. Samples
are \textbf{nested}: if \(s<t\), then \(Y_t\subseteq Y_s\). This is a classical
coverage-function random set \citep{goodman,molchanov}; related shared-level
constructions appear in uncertain-contour analysis \citep{poethkow,bolin}.

\Cref{eq:levelset-marginals} is \textbf{an identity internal to \(Q_p\)},
not evidence that \(p_i\) equals the true marginal or that true labels are
comonotone. The working posterior is attractive because it generates nested
candidates cheaply; it can be inaccurate when ambiguity changes topology,
moves components independently, or is poorly represented by the probability
map. Geometrically, the shared scalar sweeps a coordinated family of candidate
surfaces through the probability landscape. This makes \(Q_p\) a tractable
probe of boundary sensitivity, not a uniquely correct boundary posterior and
not a construction equally suited to every regional criterion.
\Cref{fig:framework} summarizes how the working posterior enters the
selective pipeline without changing the deployed action.

\subsection{Expected deployment cost as a threshold integral}

\begin{proposition}[Threshold-integral risk]
\label{prop:integral}
For any deployment cost satisfying \Cref{eq:bounded-assumption},
\begin{equation*}
  r_{L,Q}(x)
  :=\Ex_{Y\sim Q_{p(x)}}\!\left[L_x(Y,\Yhat(x))\right]
  =\int_0^1 L_x(Y_t,\Yhat(x))\,dt .
\end{equation*}
\end{proposition}
This follows immediately by integrating over the uniform threshold \(T\).
The identity is exact for the stated working posterior and does not depend on
the algebraic form of \(L\). It does not assert equality with the true
conditional risk.

Let \(H_{L,x}(t):=L_x(Y_t,\Yhat(x))\). For nodes \(t_m\in(0,1)\) and
nonnegative weights \(w_m\) summing to one, define
\begin{equation}
  r_{L,M}(x)=\sum_{m=1}^M w_m H_{L,x}(t_m),
  \qquad
  C_{L,M}(x)=-r_{L,M}(x).
  \label{eq:quad}
\end{equation}
Every candidate is compared with the fixed deployed mask. By contrast,
averaging \(L(Y_{t_m},Y_{t_n})\) over pairs of level sets estimates posterior
dispersion, not conditional risk of the deployed action.

Our default is equal-width midpoint quadrature:
\(t_m=(m-1/2)/M\) and \(w_m=1/M\), for \(m=1,\ldots,M\).
\Cref{alg:risk-aligned-confidence} shows the complete computation.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Uniform-midpoint risk-aligned confidence}
\label{alg:risk-aligned-confidence}
\small
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require probability map \(p\), deployed rule \(A_\gamma\), deployment cost \(L_x\), \(M\in\mathbb N_+\)
\Ensure scalar confidence \(C_{L,M}\), where a larger value means lower estimated risk
\State \(\Yhat\gets A_\gamma(p)\) \Comment{fix the action once before integration}
\State Construct uniform midpoint nodes \(t_m=(m-1/2)/M\), \(m=1,\ldots,M\)
\State \(r\gets0\) \Comment{initialize the working-posterior risk}
\For{\(m=1,\ldots,M\)}
  \State \(Y_m\gets\{i:p_i\geq t_m\}\) \Comment{form one nested candidate mask}
  \State \(\ell_m\gets L_x(Y_m,\Yhat)\) \Comment{evaluate the same deployed action}
  \State \(r\gets r+\ell_m/M\) \Comment{average cost under the working posterior}
\EndFor
\State \Return \(C_{L,M}\gets-r\) \Comment{negate risk so higher means safer}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The \textbf{three threshold roles are distinct}. The deployment threshold \(\gamma\)
fixes the action; \(T\sim\operatorname{Unif}(0,1)\) defines \(Q_p\); and the
\(t_m\)'s are deterministic integration nodes. Indeed, if \(T\) instead has
cumulative distribution function (CDF)
\(F\), then \(\Prb(i\in Y_T)=F(p_i)\). Preserving the coverage identity for
arbitrary probability maps therefore forces \(F(u)=u\), making the uniform law
the unique distribution-free choice. Nonuniform proposal thresholds can still
estimate the same integral with inverse-density weights; without those weights
they define a different working posterior. \Cref{app:importance-thresholds}
gives the unbiased estimator, its variance, and a defensive-mixture bound.

On a finite image \(H_{L,x}\) is a bounded step function and therefore has
finite total variation \(V(H_{L,x})\). The one-dimensional Koksma--Hlawka
inequality and midpoint star discrepancy give
\(\left|r_{L,M}(x)-r_{L,Q}(x)\right|\leq V(H_{L,x})/(2M)\).
We use midpoint nodes as a fixed numerical rule, not because the bound proves
they optimize finite-sample AURC \citep{niederreiter1992}.

\subsection{Numerical error and posterior discrepancy}
\label{sec:error}

Let \(P_x=\Prb(Y\in\cdot\mid X=x)\) denote the true label posterior. For the
deployed action define the deployment-cost discrepancy
\begin{equation}
  \Delta_{L,\Yhat}(x)
  :=
  \left|
    \Ex_{Y\sim P_x}L_x(Y,\Yhat(x))
    -
    \Ex_{Y\sim Q_{p(x)}}L_x(Y,\Yhat(x))
  \right|.
  \label{eq:loss-discrepancy}
\end{equation}
This cost-functional discrepancy is weaker than total-variation (TV) control: if
\(\TV(P_x,Q_{p(x)})\leq\delta_x\), using the supremum-over-events convention,
then \(\Delta_{L,\Yhat}(x)\leq B_x\delta_x\), while the converse need not hold.
For a fixed action \(A\), let
\(\mu_{x,A}=(L_x(\cdot,A))_\#P_x\) and
\(\nu_{x,A}=(L_x(\cdot,A))_\#Q_{p(x)}\) be the induced scalar cost laws.
\Cref{app:lem:loss-pushforward} gives the concrete bound
\(\Delta_{L,A}(x)\leq W_1(\mu_{x,A},\nu_{x,A})\). This remains available for
HD95 without pretending that its percentile surface cost is a mask metric.

\begin{proposition}[Conditional total-error bound]
\label{prop:total-bound}
For midpoint quadrature and any bounded measurable \(L\),
\begin{equation}
  \left|C_{L,M}(x)-C_L^\star(x)\right|
  \leq
  \frac{V(H_{L,x})}{2M}
  +\Delta_{L,\Yhat}(x).
  \label{eq:decomp}
\end{equation}
Under the stronger sufficient condition
\(\TV(P_x,Q_{p(x)})\leq\delta_x\), the second term can be replaced by
\(B_x\delta_x\).
\end{proposition}
The proof inserts \(r_{L,Q}(x)\) between the quadrature risk and true
conditional risk and applies the triangle inequality; \Cref{app:theory}
gives the full statement.

\begin{corollary}[Population total error]
\label{cor:population-bound}
If \(\Ex[V(H_{L,X})]<\infty\) and
\(\Ex[\Delta_{L,\Yhat}(X)]<\infty\), then
\begin{equation*}
  \Ex\!\left[
    \left|C_{L,M}(X)-C_L^\star(X)\right|
  \right]
  \leq
  \frac{\Ex[V(H_{L,X})]}{2M}
  +\Ex[\Delta_{L,\Yhat}(X)].
\end{equation*}
\end{corollary}

Increasing \(M\) \textbf{shrinks only the quadrature term}; it leaves \(p\), its coupling,
and posterior discrepancy unchanged.

\noindent\textbf{From score error to AURC.}
Separate the actual mean score error from its decomposable upper bound:
\(\eta_M:=\Ex|C_{L,M}(X)-C_L^\star(X)|\) and
\(e_M:=\Ex[V(H_{L,X})]/(2M)+\Ex[\Delta_{L,\Yhat}(X)]\), so
\(\eta_M\leq e_M\) by \Cref{cor:population-bound}. With randomized ties,
\(C_L^\star\) gives the optimal coverage-\(c\) selector, and
\Cref{app:theory} proves the transparent plug-in bound
\begin{equation*}
  0\leq R_L(c;C_{L,M})-R_L^\star(c)
  \leq\min\{B,\eta_M/c\}
  \leq\min\{B,e_M/c\}.
\end{equation*}
Integrating over coverage gives
\begin{equation*}
  0\leq\AURC_L(C_{L,M})-\AURC_L(C_L^\star)
  \leq\Psi_B(\eta_M)\leq\Psi_B(e_M),
\end{equation*}
where \(\Psi_B(z)=z[1+\log(B/z)]\) for \(0<z<B\), with
\(\Psi_B(0)=0\) and \(\Psi_B(z)=B\) for \(z\geq B\). Thus quadrature and
posterior errors control \textbf{population AURC regret}: low coverage is the
most sensitive pointwise regime, yet its integral remains finite. The appendix
also proves the coverage-independent bound \(\min\{B,2\epsilon\}\) under
uniform score error \(\epsilon\). The factor one in the \(L^1\) bound is not a
missing factor: it follows from comparing the two randomized selectors before
applying the absolute-value bound.
