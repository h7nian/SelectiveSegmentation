Tables~\ref{tab:main-results}--\ref{tab:main-results-sf} use the same six
datasets, ten methods, and three risk blocks for CLIP-T, DL-T, and SF-T. These
absolute AURCs are a symmetric descriptive display. The claims below retain the
original locked families: ten primary target conditions and seven
architecture/domain extension conditions. DUTS/CLIP-T completes Table~1 only
and does not enlarge either inferential family.

\noindent\textbf{Worst-boundary risk.}
HD-M32 lowers HD-risk AURC relative to Dice-M32 in \textbf{all ten target conditions};
all ten pointwise intervals exclude zero. A boundary-indexed score is lowest
among the ten displayed methods in nine conditions, with SDC on
FIVES/CLIPSeg-Target the exception. HD-M32 also has higher association with
realized HD safety than Dice-M32 in all ten, with Spearman differences
.179--.432. The level-set score thus carries spatial-extreme information absent
from its regional counterpart.

\noindent\textbf{Robust boundary risk.}
HD-M32 is lower than HD95-M32 in nine conditions under HD risk; HD95-M32 is
lower in eight under HD95 risk. Only four and two matched-direction intervals
exclude zero. The latter comparison is checkpoint-sensitive: five conditions
reverse across seeds and seed 0 disagrees with the majority in three. Both
boundary macro contrasts also reverse at action threshold \(\gamma=.3\). HD95
is therefore a \textbf{useful but condition-sensitive robust endpoint}, not a guaranteed
improvement over HD.

\noindent\textbf{Regional contrast.}
Dice-M32 is lower than HD-M32 in eight conditions under Dice risk, but only
three intervals exclude zero. \textbf{Dice-M32 and SDC split their direct comparison
five conditions to five}, so the data support parity with condition-specific
wins rather than a general advantage for either score; foreground entropy also
remains competitive.
Outside FIVES/CLIPSeg-Target, Dice-M32 and foreground entropy correlate
.916--.960. Dice-M32 and Dice-Exact have \(\rho\geq.9992\),
\(\tau_b\geq.9791\), and at most .363 displayed AURC difference, so numerical
integration is not the limiting issue.
The parity with SDC is also \textbf{not explained by poorly calibrated CLIPSeg
maps alone}. FIVES/CLIPSeg-Target, where SDC has its largest advantage
(5.110 AURC \(\times100\)), has 15-bin pixel ECE .0071. Across the ten target
conditions, the descriptive Spearman correlation between ECE and
Dice-M32-minus-SDC AURC is \(-.855\): worse ECE is associated with a shift
toward Dice-M32, the opposite of that simple explanation. This small,
pixel-weighted diagnostic does not make calibration irrelevant; it instead
supports the count-dispersion account, under which two distinct functionals of
the same map can remain close even when its marginals are well calibrated.

\subsection{Ablation studies}

\noindent\textbf{How many threshold nodes are needed?}
\Cref{fig:threshold-count-ablation} compares \(M\in\{2,8,32\}\) with
Dice-Exact and the high-resolution boundary reference \(M=128\). Dice-M32 and
Dice-Exact have \(\rho\geq.9992\), \(\tau_b\geq.9791\), and at most .363
displayed AURC difference. For HD and HD95, M32--M128 Spearman agreement is
.973--.998 and .951--.996, respectively. \textbf{M32 already recovers nearly the
same ranking}; more nodes refine the integral but do not resolve posterior
misspecification.

\noindent\textbf{Does the deployed action change the conclusion?}
\Cref{fig:gamma-sensitivity} recomputes both the fixed action and every
action-aware score at \(\gamma\in\{.3,.5,.7\}\). The HD-over-Dice advantage
under HD risk persists at all three thresholds. By contrast, both HD--HD95
macro comparisons cross zero at \(\gamma=.3\). The flagship boundary result is
therefore action-robust in this range, whereas the distinction between maximum
and percentile boundary risk is action-sensitive.

\noindent\textbf{Does training randomness change the conclusion?}
The three-checkpoint audit in \Cref{fig:seed-sensitivity} preserves the
macro direction of Dice-M32 versus HD-M32. The two boundary contrasts are less
stable: five conditions reverse across checkpoints and the primary seed
disagrees with the within-condition majority in three. This distinction keeps
the main HD claim separate from the more tentative HD--HD95 ordering.

\noindent\textbf{Does a richer Dice coupling help?}
\Cref{tab:dice-count-posterior} fixes the probability map, deployed mask,
and pixel marginals, then replaces one global threshold by independent
thresholds inside and outside the action. Its mean/median gain over Dice-M32 is
only .0195/.0020 AURC \(\times100\). No component-grid variant passes its fixed
gate or beats SDC or foreground entropy in more than 6/10 conditions, and the
three-checkpoint Ensemble-Q loses to LevelSet-Q in 9/10. Together with the
five--five Dice-M32/SDC split, these \textbf{negative ablations support parity and
the count-law explanation}, not superiority of either Dice score.

The count-dispersion inequality holds for every nonempty action at machine
precision. It is deliberately conservative: across the ten target conditions,
the condition-level median absolute SDC--Exact risk difference ranges from
.022 to 12.048 on the \(\times100\) scale, while the corresponding median
bound ranges from 11.765 to 805.692. Thus the theorem explains \emph{when}
concentration makes SDC and posterior Dice close, but is not a useful numerical
certificate for small or diffuse predictions. Empty actions are excluded from
the \(1/|\Yhat|\) bound and reported separately in
\Cref{tab:sdc-bound-audit}.

\noindent\textbf{Does smooth non-nested coupling help?}
The prespecified spatial copula improves on Dice-M32 in 5/10 conditions and
worsens it in five, so it \textbf{fails the declared 7/10 directional gate}.
Its macro difference is \(-.665\) AURC\(\times100\), but the median is only
\(-.020\): the mean is dominated by FIVES/CLIP-T, where the difference is
\(-5.001\) with pointwise interval \([-6.404,-3.373]\). Both TN3K conditions
also improve directionally, whereas both ISIC conditions worsen with intervals
excluding zero. Thus non-nested dependence is useful for particular regional
failure modes, not a universal Dice posterior. The primary four-repeat AURC
range has median/max .102/.334, so Monte Carlo noise is smaller than the large
condition effects. A finer-scale sensitivity variant wins 7/10, but it was not
the prespecified primary setting and is not promoted post hoc.

\noindent\textbf{What changes when the true coupling is known?}
The complete synthetic matrix separates geometry from posterior adequacy.
All three available matched controls recover their conditional risks and
AURC ordering exactly. Under independent truth, using the shared-threshold
estimator incurs MAE .0188 for Dice, .0623 for HD, and .0446 for HD95; under
local-block truth the corresponding errors are .0186, .0455, and .0464.
Thus boundary risks are more sensitive to this coupling mismatch in these
controlled families, but \textbf{no coupling is universally privileged}.
The deliberately unmatched bimodal truth makes the distinction sharper:
independent coupling has the lowest Dice-risk MAE (.0260), while shared
threshold has the lowest Dice AURC regret (.032 on the \(\times100\) scale).
Likewise, spatial copula has the lowest HD95-risk MAE (.0254), whereas shared
threshold has the lowest HD95 AURC regret (.095). Absolute risk estimation and
selective ordering are therefore related but not interchangeable, and a
loss-compatible transport geometry does not establish that a chosen
working posterior is true. The full matrix is reported in
\Cref{tab:synthetic-coupling-matrix,fig:synthetic-coupling-matrix}.

\noindent\textbf{Diagnostics.}
Known-posterior tests show TV near one with Dice score error only .0135--.0393,
while support
checks find incompatible references on up to 11.5\% of FIVES and 15.4\% of
ISIC. The HD ranking result is strong, but \textbf{one annotation per image cannot
validate a nested conditional posterior}. \Cref{app:additional-results}
reports all AURCs, intervals, ablations, curves, and stress tests.
