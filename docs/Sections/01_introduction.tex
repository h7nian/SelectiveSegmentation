\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

A segmentation system deployed with human oversight must decide which predictions
can be accepted and which should be deferred. Average test accuracy does not answer
this question. Two systems with the same average Dice can have different practical
value if one reliably defers its failures while the other assigns those failures high
confidence. Selective prediction captures this requirement: a fixed model emits a
prediction \(\widehat Y(x)\) and an image-level confidence \(g(x)\); predictions are
accepted from high to low confidence, and the induced ranking is evaluated by a
risk--coverage curve \citep{geifman2017selective,selectivenet,feng,jaeger}.

The risk in that curve is application-dependent. Dice and IoU measure overlap,
whereas HD95 and related distances measure boundary behavior. Moving a small
component far from the target can change little in overlap while producing a large
surface error; a diffuse boundary error can have the opposite profile. Consequently,
a confidence score cannot be called correct without specifying the loss under
which accepted predictions are judged.

Most single-pass post-hoc scores aggregate a per-pixel quantity such as maximum
probability or entropy. Systematic studies show that aggregation is itself a major
design choice \citep{values,zenk}. Soft Dice Confidence (SDC) takes a more direct
route: it aligns confidence with the Dice objective and provides an efficient
Dice-specific marginal surrogate \citep{sdc}. Its success motivates a broader
question: can the same metric-alignment principle produce a post-hoc confidence for
an arbitrary bounded segmentation loss while exposing the posterior assumptions it
requires?

We answer by separating three objects that are often conflated. The
\emph{deployed action} \(\widehat Y(x)\) is the mask actually accepted or rejected;
it may be obtained at threshold \(0.5\), threshold \(0.3\), or by another fixed
decision rule. The \emph{deployment loss} \(L\) specifies the harm of accepting an
incorrect mask. Finally, evaluating the conditional risk of that action before its
label is observed requires a distribution over possible labels. A probability map
supplies one value per pixel, but not the joint distribution of an entire mask.

We make the third object explicit through a classical random-set construction. A
single uniform threshold applied to the probability map generates a nested family of
candidate masks. This preserves the map as the one-pixel coverage function and turns
expected loss into a one-dimensional threshold integral
\citep{goodman,molchanov}. We call the resulting law a \emph{working posterior}: it
is computable and its dependence structure is visible, but it is not asserted to be
the true label posterior.

For quadrature nodes \(t_m\) and weights \(w_m\), the construction gives
\begin{equation}
  C_{L,M}(x)
  =-\sum_{m=1}^M w_m
    L\!\left(Y_{t_m},\widehat Y(x)\right).
  \label{eq:intro-score}
\end{equation}
Only \(L\) changes between an overlap confidence and a boundary confidence. Every
candidate label is compared with the same deployed mask; pairwise dispersion among
candidate labels is a different estimand.

This formulation also prevents an overly strong interpretation of numerical
convergence. Increasing \(M\) improves approximation of expected loss under the
working posterior. It cannot repair a miscalibrated probability map or an incorrect
joint model. Our total-error analysis therefore contains a deterministic quadrature
term and a separate, loss-specific posterior discrepancy.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item We define loss-indexed selective risk \(\AURC_L\) and its Bayes-optimal
  image-level confidence \(C_L^\star\), making both the deployed action and its
  evaluation loss explicit.
  \item We introduce a unified level-set estimator for any bounded measurable
  segmentation loss. Under its stated working posterior the target is exact, and
  midpoint quadrature admits a deterministic \(O(M^{-1})\) error bound separated
  from posterior-model error.
  \item We characterize the relation to SDC: SDC is a marginal-only ratio of
  expected Dice components, whereas the level-set Dice score is an expected Dice
  under an explicit joint law. Neither set of probabilistic assumptions implies the
  other.
  \item We evaluate ten fixed model--dataset conditions with tie-aware AURC and
  paired, multiplicity-controlled inference. The nHD95 score wins its boundary-risk
  comparison in all ten conditions, while the Dice-risk comparison splits six to
  four and SDC remains the strongest primary Dice-risk score in six conditions.
\end{enumerate}

The empirical conclusion is deliberately non-universal: deployment loss is a useful
index for constructing and auditing confidence, but matching the confidence and risk
by name does not guarantee the best ranking.
