\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

A binary segmenter used with human oversight must answer two questions. First,
what mask will it deploy? Second, when should that mask be trusted? The second
question has no metric-free answer. A contour can cover nearly the right region
while placing one component far away, or have one extreme boundary error while
the rest of its surface is accurate. Dice, full Hausdorff distance (HD), and
HD95 tell these three stories differently: region overlap, worst boundary
displacement, and boundary displacement after discounting the most extreme
tail. A confidence score that ranks one story well need not rank the others.

We therefore index confidence by the loss that matters after deployment. For a
fixed action \(\Yhat(x)\) and loss \(L\), the ideal confidence is negative
conditional risk,
\[
  C_L^\star(x)=-\Ex[L(Y,\Yhat(x))\mid X=x].
\]
This definition is simple; computing it is not. A model probability map supplies
pixelwise scores intended to estimate foreground marginals, but even exact true
pixelwise marginals would not identify the joint label law on which nonlinear
mask losses depend.

Our construction makes the missing law visible. Given a foreground map \(p\),
draw one shared threshold \(T\sim\operatorname{Unif}(0,1)\) and set
\(Y_T=\{i:p_i\geq T\}\). The resulting nested random masks preserve \(p\) as
their coverage function and turn working-posterior risk into the one-dimensional
integral \(\int_0^1L(Y_t,\Yhat)\,dt\)
\citep{goodman,molchanov}. Midpoint quadrature gives one estimator for any
bounded mask loss; for Dice, the integral also has an exact finite level-set
algorithm. This is a declared \emph{working posterior}, not a claim that the
true annotation law is nested.

That distinction drives the theory. Score error separates into numerical
integration error and loss-specific posterior discrepancy, and population score
error in turn controls selective-risk and AURC regret. A general
Lipschitz--Wasserstein principle then gives geometry-aware sufficient conditions.
Dice and normalized penalized full-surface HD instantiate the same template, but
under different ground geometries---Jaccard distance for regional overlap and
surface Hausdorff distance for worst-case boundary error. These assumptions have
the same form, not the same numerical content, and neither generally implies the
other. Pooled nHD95 is a useful robust percentile extension in the bounded-loss
framework, but it is nonmetric and does not inherit the HD-specific Wasserstein
corollary.

The empirical study follows the same progression:
\[
  \text{Dice (region)}\quad\longrightarrow\quad
  \text{nHD (worst boundary)}\quad\longrightarrow\quad
  \text{nHD95 (robust boundary tail)}.
\]
Across five native-binary datasets and sixteen fixed model--dataset conditions,
we hold each probability map and deployed mask fixed, then evaluate the complete
\(3\times3\) matrix of confidence loss and evaluation loss. The study asks two
adjacent questions: does the preferred ranking change from region to worst-case
boundary risk, and does it change again when the most extreme boundary tail is
made robust? No same-named score is presumed to win.

\subsection{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Selective prediction and segmentation confidence.}
Selective prediction formalizes risk--coverage trade-offs
\citep{geifman2017selective,selectivenet,feng}, while failure-detection studies
show that evaluation loss and aggregation can alter conclusions
\citep{jaeger,values}. Segmentation work aggregates pixel uncertainty globally,
over predicted regions, or over patches \citep{values,zenk}; recent comparisons
again find the best aggregation to be dataset dependent
\citep{guarino2026aggregation}. We expose the deployment loss inside AURC rather
than treating image-level confidence as universal.

\paragraph{SDC, random sets, and boundary geometry.}
SDC aligns a single-map score with Dice by computing soft Dice between the
probability map and deployed mask \citep{sdc}. Our Dice score instead averages
a nonlinear mask loss under an explicit coupling: it is an expectation of a
ratio, whereas SDC is a ratio of expectations. Random level sets are classical
coverage-function constructions \citep{goodman,molchanov} and appear in contour
and excursion-set analysis \citep{poethkow,bolin}. Boundary measures expose
errors that overlap can miss \citep{karimi2020}; we use full HD and pooled HD95
to distinguish worst-case from robust percentile boundary behavior.

\subsection{Our Contributions}

We make four contributions. First, we define loss-indexed confidence and AURC
for a fixed deployed mask, then construct a common level-set estimator for any
bounded measurable segmentation loss. Second, we connect quadrature and
posterior discrepancy to population score error, selective-risk regret, and
AURC regret. Third, we derive geometry-aware Wasserstein specializations for
Dice and normalized full-surface HD, add a fixed-action Dice
overlap--cardinality decomposition, clarify why pooled nHD95 has only the
generic guarantee, and give an exact \(O(N\log N)\) level-set Dice algorithm.
Fourth, we conduct a locked five-dataset, sixteen-condition experiment with
seventeen single-map scores, a complete three-loss cross-evaluation, tie-aware
AURC, and pointwise paired-bootstrap intervals for four fixed adjacent-geometry
comparisons.
