\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

A segmentation can overlap almost perfectly with the annotation yet contain a
small component far from the anatomy. Dice may barely change, full Hausdorff
distance (HD) calls the error catastrophic, and its 95th-percentile variant
(HD95) may suppress it when fewer than roughly five percent of pooled surface
distances are extreme. \textbf{The same deployed mask can therefore be safe
under one loss and unsafe under another.}

This exposes a gap in segmentation confidence. Most one-map scores compress
pixel uncertainty by a mean, entropy, patch, or overlap statistic. Such a score
may rank failures well, but it usually leaves two questions implicit:
\textbf{which deployment loss defines failure, and which joint law over masks
turns the probability map into conditional risk?} Pixelwise marginals alone do
not answer the second question for nonlinear region or boundary losses.

We make both choices explicit. First freeze the deployed hard mask. Then draw a
shared threshold through its probability map to obtain coherent nested candidate
masks. Finally compare every candidate with the same deployed action under the
stated loss and average those losses. The result estimates the negative
conditional risk of the action, rather than generic posterior spread. The
shared-threshold law preserves the probability map as a coverage function
\citep{goodman,molchanov}; it is a transparent \emph{working posterior}, not a
claim that the unknown annotation posterior is nested.

Our flagship loss is normalized penalized full HD (nHD), which records the
worst normalized surface displacement and assigns a fixed penalty to one-sided
empty masks. Here coherent level-set motion and the loss share the same surface
geometry, yielding a constant-one Wasserstein posterior bound. We propagate
score error to area under the risk--coverage curve (AURC) regret. Normalized
penalized HD95 (nHD95) instead uses the 95th percentile of the pooled
bidirectional surface distances: it is a robust practical extension, but its
percentile operation is nonmetric. Dice provides the regional contrast, and
Soft Dice Confidence (SDC) reveals why a count-oriented one-map score can remain
hard to beat.

We \textbf{freeze every probability map and deployed action}, then change only confidence and
evaluation loss. The primary question is whether boundary-aligned confidence
improves selection under worst-boundary risk. We next compare full and robust
boundary loss, then use Dice to expose where the shared-threshold coupling is
less informative. Five datasets, two model families, sixteen conditions, and a
complete \(3\times3\) score--risk design separate these questions.

\subsection{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\noindent\textbf{Selective prediction and failure detection.}
Selective classification formalizes abstention through the risk--coverage
tradeoff: a system accepts high-confidence inputs and pays task loss only on the
accepted set \citep{geifman2017selective,selectivenet,feng}. Subsequent work
emphasizes that apparent progress in failure detection can depend on the score,
error definition, and summary measure used for evaluation \citep{jaeger}. This
issue is sharper in segmentation. Classification normally fixes one zero--one
error, whereas a mask can be judged by overlap, worst boundary displacement, or
a robust surface percentile. We retain the risk--coverage and AURC formalism,
but index both quantities by the deployed segmentation loss. The resulting
question is \textbf{not merely whether a prediction is uncertain}; it is whether the
fixed deployed mask is risky under a specified notion of geometric harm.

\noindent\textbf{Segmentation quality control.}
Existing quality-control methods span several information budgets. Reverse
Classification Accuracy (RCA) fits a reverse model for each test prediction and
evaluates it against an annotated reference set \citep{valindria2017rca}.
Learned quality estimators may regress observed mask quality from validation
labels or intermediate features. Validation of Uncertainty Estimation for
Segmentation (ValUES) systematizes uncertainty evaluation in
semantic segmentation, while medical-segmentation benchmarking shows that the
conversion from pixel uncertainty to an image-level failure score can matter as
much as the underlying uncertainty estimator \citep{values,zenk}. These are
important deployment strategies, but an atlas, labelled calibration set, learned
quality head, or repeated forward passes changes what information is available.
Our primary study deliberately fixes a narrower budget: \textbf{one probability map,
one hard action, and no fitted quality predictor}. RCA and learned regressors are
therefore related alternatives rather than matched baselines.

\noindent\textbf{Aggregating one probability map.}
Even at the single-map budget, spatial aggregation is not unique. Global mean
entropy and mean maximum probability discard where uncertainty occurs. Recent
spatially aware schemes instead select a foreground-sized high-uncertainty
fraction or the most uncertain local patch \citep{guarino2026aggregation}; the
median--minimum--maximum confidence (MMMC) statistic summarizes extrema and the
median of pixel uncertainty \citep{holder2021uncertainty}. We include the
quantile foreground-ratio score (QFR-Entropy), the rank-equivalent patch-level
mean/aggregation score (PLM/PLA-10-Entropy), MMMC-Entropy, foreground entropy,
and the global summaries
as fixed references. Their common object is a \textbf{scalar aggregation of pixelwise
uncertainty}. Our score instead generates candidate masks, compares each candidate
with the same deployed action under \(L\), and aggregates the resulting
structured losses. This difference lets a boundary score retain the location and
extent of an uncertain surface excursion rather than only its entropy mass.

\noindent\textbf{Sampling agreement and threshold stability.}
Monte Carlo dropout and deep ensembles obtain multiple predictive maps through
additional stochastic or independently trained forward passes
\citep{gal2016dropout,lakshminarayanan2017ensembles}. Bayesian QuickNAT uses
agreement across dropout segmentations, including overlap-based summaries, for
structure-wise quality control \citep{roy2019bayesianquicknat}. A different
single-map stability idea appears in the Segment Anything Model (SAM), which
compares masks obtained after a
small shift of its binarization cutoff \citep{kirillov2023segmentanything}.
Our level-set expectation is related to both ideas but is \textbf{identical to neither}:
it integrates an explicitly declared continuum law over thresholds rather than
using two nearby masks or treating model members as annotation samples. We
include threshold intersection-over-union (threshold-IoU) at the single-map
budget and separately label the
three-checkpoint empirical posterior at its higher inference budget. Model
disagreement can test support and ranking mechanisms, but it does not by itself
identify human annotation variability.

\noindent\textbf{Random sets and boundary geometry.}
Coverage functions and random level sets have a long history in fuzzy-set and
random-set theory \citep{goodman,molchanov}. Shared-level constructions also
appear in probabilistic surface extraction and excursion-set analysis
\citep{poethkow,bolin}. We use the shared threshold as a \textbf{transparent comonotone
coupling} of pixel marginals: it produces coherent nested masks and makes the
otherwise implicit joint assumption auditable. Boundary-aware objectives and
metrics were developed precisely because overlap can hide spatially remote
errors \citep{karimi2020}. Our nHD instantiation connects these two strands:
level sets supply a simple boundary-motion posterior, while normalized full
Hausdorff distance supplies the matching mask geometry. Pooled nHD95 remains a
practical robust endpoint, but its percentile operation prevents the same metric
argument.

\noindent\textbf{The closest metric-aligned predecessor.}
Soft Dice Confidence (SDC) directly aligns a one-map score with Dice by computing
soft Dice between \(p\) and \(\Yhat\), together with a Dice-specific
approximation analysis \citep{sdc}. It is our closest predecessor and a strong
baseline. \textbf{The distinction is probabilistic rather than cosmetic}: SDC is a ratio
of expected overlap and size counts, whereas level-set Dice is the expectation
of their random ratio under a declared coupling. Our count-dispersion theorem
explains when those quantities are close, and the negative coupling ablations
show why replacing SDC is difficult in practice. The broader framework is not a
claim that level sets dominate SDC for regional risk; it uses the same
loss-indexed principle beyond Dice, with its clearest advantage under Hausdorff
boundary risk.

\subsection{Our Contributions}

We contribute: (i) loss-indexed confidence and a shared-threshold estimator for
any bounded segmentation loss; (ii) an nHD flagship specialization linking
coherent boundary motion to a constant-one surface-Wasserstein bound; (iii) a
complete error chain from quadrature and posterior discrepancy to AURC regret,
with nHD95 and Dice specializations, Exact Dice, and an SDC count-dispersion
bound; and (iv) a locked five-dataset study with full cross-loss evaluation,
single-map baselines, paired uncertainty, action and checkpoint stress tests,
and separately labelled higher-budget posterior diagnostics.
