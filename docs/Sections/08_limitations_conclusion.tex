\section{Limitations and Conclusion}
\label{sec:limitations}
\label{sec:limitations-conclusion}

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The level-set law is one coupling compatible with a probability map, not a
posterior identified by calibrated marginals. Exact Dice removes its numerical
integration error, and larger \(M\) reduces midpoint error for the other losses,
but neither operation removes posterior discrepancy. The score-error theorem
therefore controls population AURC regret only to the extent that its discrepancy
term is small; it neither guarantees pairwise rank recovery nor says that a
matched score must win empirically.
Exact cardinality diagnostics expose direct support incompatibilities---most
clearly on FIVES and ISIC---but pooled PIT and bias summaries remain
finite-sample evidence, and one label per image cannot turn favorable
diagnostics into validation of \(Q_p\).

Dice and normalized full HD share a Lipschitz--Wasserstein proof template, but
their Jaccard and surface-Hausdorff agreement conditions express different
geometries and are not interchangeable. Surface HD is naturally a metric on the
extracted digital surfaces and, conservatively, a pseudometric on masks. Pooled
nHD95 is nonmetric: percentile and surface-multiplicity jumps prevent it from
inheriting the HD corollary without stronger regularity assumptions. It remains
covered by the generic bounded-loss, posterior-discrepancy, and AURC theory.

The boundary surface, pixel spacing, diagonal normalization, percentile pooling,
and empty-mask penalty are substantive deployment choices. Our experiment uses
native pixel coordinates and one fixed convention; physical-distance or
application-specific costs could induce another ranking. Empirically, the design
covers five binary datasets, two model families, and one training seed. The
locked \(\gamma\in\{.3,.5,.7\}\) sensitivity preserves the main
region-to-worst-boundary direction but triggers the predeclared locked warning
rule for the worst-to-robust-boundary comparison; conclusions involving nHD versus
nHD95 therefore depend on the deployed action threshold. Its
paired bootstrap captures held-out image variation for fixed fitted checkpoints,
not optimization, site-shift, or multi-rater uncertainty. Finally, all methods
use one probability map: ensembles, internal features, learned failure detectors,
multiclass actions, and absolute risk calibration remain outside scope.
Appendix~\ref{app:limitations} expands these constraints.

\paragraph{Conclusion.}
\label{sec:conclusion}
Selective segmentation begins with a practical choice: which failure matters
when a mask is accepted? Loss indexing turns that choice into an explicit
confidence target. The shared-threshold working posterior makes the otherwise
hidden joint assumption inspectable, the theory traces its error to selective
risk and AURC, and the three-loss design follows a coherent geometric path from
region overlap to worst-case and robust boundary error. The framework offers a
common way to construct and compare these rankings; it deliberately does not
promise that one geometry, posterior approximation, or same-named score is best
for every deployment.
