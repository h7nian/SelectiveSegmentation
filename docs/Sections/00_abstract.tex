\begin{abstract}
Selective segmentation must decide not only what mask to deploy, but also which
predictions are safe to accept. We make that second decision explicit by indexing
confidence by the deployment loss: the ideal score is the negative conditional
risk of the fixed deployed mask. Because pixelwise marginals do not identify this
quantity for nonlinear mask losses, we state a shared-threshold level-set law as a
\emph{working posterior}, rather than treating it as the true label posterior. This
turns expected structured loss into a one-dimensional threshold integral. We
separate quadrature error from posterior discrepancy and show how population score
error controls coverage-wise selective-risk and AURC regret. Dice and normalized
Hausdorff (nHD) losses then instantiate the same Lipschitz--Wasserstein principle
under different geometries---Jaccard overlap and normalized surface Hausdorff
distance, respectively---while nHD95 is a robust, nonmetric boundary extension
covered by the general bounded-loss theory but not by the nHD-specific corollary.
For Dice, an exact level-set algorithm removes quadrature error altogether. We
evaluate this geometry-indexed view on five binary-segmentation datasets and
sixteen fixed model--dataset conditions using a complete \(3\times3\) design:
Dice-, nHD-, and nHD95-indexed confidence are each evaluated under all three
deployment risks, with predeclared adjacent-geometry comparisons and fixed
baselines. On the ten target-adapted conditions, nHD-M32 lowers nHD-risk AURC
relative to Dice-M32 in all ten, whereas Dice-M32 lowers Dice-risk AURC in eight;
nHD-versus-nHD95 effects are smaller and condition dependent.
\end{abstract}
