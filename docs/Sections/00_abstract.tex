\begin{abstract}
Selective segmentation requires more than an accurate mask: a system must also rank
its predictions by the harm they would incur if accepted, and that ranking depends on
the deployment loss. We formalize this dependence by indexing both selective risk
and confidence by a bounded segmentation loss \(L\). The ideal score is the negative
conditional risk of the mask actually deployed,
\(C_L^\star(x)=-\mathbb{E}[L(Y,\widehat Y(x))\mid X=x]\).
Because a single probability map does not specify the required joint label posterior,
we introduce a level-set law as an explicit \emph{working posterior}: draw one
threshold \(T\sim\mathrm{Unif}(0,1)\) and set
\(Y_T=\{i:p_i\geq T\}\). Under this stated model, expected loss is exactly the
one-dimensional integral \(\int_0^1 L(Y_t,\widehat Y)\,dt\), yielding one
\(M\)-point quadrature estimator for overlap and boundary losses. We separate its
deterministic quadrature error from a loss-specific discrepancy between the working
and true posteriors. For Dice, the resulting score is an expectation of a ratio,
whereas Soft Dice Confidence is a ratio of expectations. Their assumptions are
therefore not ordered: our construction is more general in the deployment loss, but
not uniformly weaker probabilistically. Across ten fixed model--dataset conditions,
nHD95-M32 outranks Dice-M32 under nHD95 risk in all ten, whereas Dice-M32 wins their
Dice-risk comparison in six and loses in four; SDC has the lowest Dice-risk AURC
among the five primary scores in six conditions. Thus loss indexing exposes useful
boundary-specific rankings, but metric matching alone does not imply universal
superiority.
\end{abstract}
